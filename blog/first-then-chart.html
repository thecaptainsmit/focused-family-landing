First-Then Chart for ADHD Kids: The Complete Guide (Free Template)

Published March 12, 2026 · 6 min read · ADHD Strategies

"Time to leave the playground!" Cue screaming, kicking, and a 20-minute meltdown. Sound familiar? For kids with ADHD, transitions are hard. Really hard. But there's a simple visual tool that can reduce these battles by up to 70%: the First-Then chart.

What Is a First-Then Chart?

A First-Then chart is a visual support tool that breaks activities into two parts: what comes FIRST, then what comes next. It's deceptively simple but incredibly powerful for ADHD brains.

Instead of saying "Clean up your room" (overwhelming), you show:

  • FIRST: Put toys in bin
  • THEN: Play outside

The "then" is the reward — something motivating that comes right after the less-preferred activity. This creates a clear path through transitions.

Why First-Then Works for ADHD

1. Reduces Cognitive Load

ADHD brains struggle with working memory — holding information in mind while doing something else. A First-Then chart externalizes this. The information is visible, not stored in their head.

2. Provides Predictability

Kids with ADHD often feel anxious about transitions because they don't know what's coming. First-Then removes that uncertainty. They can see the whole sequence.

3. Creates Motivation

The "then" activity acts as a natural reward. This taps into the dopamine system — exactly what ADHD brains need to initiate tasks.

4. Reduces Verbal Battles

Instead of repeating yourself 47 times, you simply point to the chart. The chart becomes the "bad guy," not you. This preserves your relationship and reduces conflict.

How to Create Your First-Then Chart

Materials Needed

  • Paper or cardstock
  • Marker (thick, dark)
  • Pictures (drawn or printed)
  • Laminator (optional but recommended)

Step-by-Step Instructions

Step 1: Divide your paper in half. Label left side "FIRST" and right side "THEN" in large, bold letters.

Step 2: Add a picture for the FIRST activity (the less-preferred task). Use simple drawings or photos.

Step 3: Add a picture for the THEN activity (the reward/motivator). Make this image bigger or more colorful.

Step 4: Laminate or use a page protector. You'll use this hundreds of times.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Mistake #1: Making the FIRST too long

"Clean your room" as the FIRST will fail. It's too big. Instead: "Put 5 toys away." Small, specific, achievable.

Mistake #2: Using it inconsistently

First-Then only works if you use it every time for that transition. Inconsistency creates confusion and battles.

Mistake #3: Forgetting the THEN

If you don't follow through with the reward, the system breaks. Your child won't trust it next time.

Mistake #4: Using negative THENs

"First homework, then chores" won't motivate anyone. The THEN must be something they want.

The Research Behind First-Then

First-Then charts are based on premack principle — a behavioral science concept that states: high-probability behaviors (preferred activities) can be used to reinforce low-probability behaviors (non-preferred activities).

Studies show visual supports like First-Then charts reduce transition time by 40-70% in children with autism and ADHD. They're recommended by the CDC and CHADD as a first-line behavioral intervention.

Advanced First-Then Strategies

Once you've mastered basic First-Then, try these advanced techniques:

First-Then-After

For older kids, add a third step: First: Homework → Then: Snack → After: Video games

First-Then with Choices

Give control: First: Get dressed → Then: Your choice: park OR play dough

First-Then Chain

Link multiple activities: First-Then becomes First(1)-Then(1) which is First(2) for the next Then(2).

Start Using First-Then Today

Pick ONE transition that always causes battles. Create a simple First-Then chart. Use it consistently for one week. Track what happens.

Most parents see improvement within 3-5 days. The key is consistency and making the THEN genuinely motivating.
